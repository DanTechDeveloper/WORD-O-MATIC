# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

Primary: elementary students (K-5) building vocabulary through play. They
arrive via a 4-digit PIN, learn through two game modes (Word Blast / read,
Story Quest / speak), and are motivated by levels, streaks, badges, and
leaderboards. The interface must read as a game console, not a worksheet.

Secondary: teachers managing classrooms. They create assignments, set
deadlines, edit word/paragraph modules, and review progress and reports.
Their surface is a denser admin tool than the student arcade, but speaks the
same visual language.

## Product Purpose

Word-O-Matic turns vocabulary practice into a game. Students progress through
10 sequential read levels and 10 sequential speak levels, earning points,
badges, and leaderboard rank as speech- and reading-recognition scores
improve. Teachers assign work and track mastery. Success looks like a student
who keeps coming back to "play" and, in doing so, masters words.

## Positioning

Practice that feels like play: every vocabulary drill is a level you want to
beat.

## Brand Personality

Playful, electric, bold. A game console for word learning — high-contrast,
physical, and loud in spirit, never quiet or corporate.

## Anti-references

- Soft, flat SaaS minimalism and the muted-cream "AI default" palette.
- Gradient-text decorative effects and glassmorphism as defaults.
- Ad-hoc neobrutalist drift: raw `zinc-*` / `purple-500` / `green-800`
  utilities, inconsistent radii, shadows that drift in color per file.
- A "Terminal" label or sci-fi framing with no terminal visual language to
  back it up.
- Anything that reads as a worksheet, test, or homework UI.

## Design Principles

- **Play first, learn second.** The vocabulary task is wrapped in arcade
  energy; motivation comes from the game, not from being told to study.
- **One loud color, many quiet ones.** Lime is the only call-to-action color;
  violet, magenta, and peach are chrome and state, never competing for the
  click.
- **Physical, not flat.** Hard offset shadows make every card and button feel
  like a pressable game piece — depth is earned by raising a surface, never by
  soft blur.
- **State drives the screen.** Locked, current, complete, and earned states
  are explicit and visible everywhere, the way the Level Card shows them.
- **Small text is a HUD.** Short, uppercase, heavy labels carry the game
  voice; never soften interface text into sentence-case body copy.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA. Body text meets ≥4.5:1 contrast against its surface;
large/display text ≥3:1. Honor `prefers-reduced-motion`: every animation has
a calm crossfade or instant alternative. Keep hit targets large and mic/speech
feedback clear, since many interactions are voice-driven.
